INTRODUCTION

The Internet has revolutionized numerous aspects of society, including telecommunications, education, entertainment, and commerce, connecting around 5.3 billion people,
or 61.4% of the global population, as of December 2023[9]. Its influence and adoption
continue to grow, making it a crucial part of daily life.
Originating from ARPANET, a late 1960s project by DARPA, the Internet evolved from
connecting just four university computers[70]. However, security concerns quickly became
apparent with the rise of malicious users.
The creation of the World Wide Web in 1989 by Tim Berners-Lee[23] marked a shift,
making the Internet a resource beyond the scientific community. From one million public
hosts in 1993, the numbers skyrocketed to 171 million in 2003 and surpassed one billion by
late 2013[1]. Unfortunately, the expansion also amplified attacker motivations, especially
with the potential for significant financial gain from exploiting security vulnerabilities. Today, the transmission and storage of sensitive and financial information across the Internet
have become commonplace, highlighting the critical need for robust security to protect data
from falling into the wrong hands.
1.1

Navigating the Cyber Threat Landscape

Cybercrime, encompassing a wide range of illegal activities involving digital devices,
computer networks, and the internet, can be perpetrated by individuals, organized groups,
or state-sponsored actors. Criminals may utilize computers as instruments for or targets
of their activities. The dynamic and rapidly evolving nature of cybercrime, fueled by the
proliferation of connected devices and easy internet access, presents significant prevention
1

challenges. While technology offers immense societal benefits, it also expands the attack
surface for criminals, lowering the barrier to entry for cybercrime. The availability of
sophisticated attack tools and anonymization techniques further complicates efforts to track
and stop cybercriminals.
It is common for cybercrime to spread across jurisdictions, which makes it more difficult for law enforcement entities to prosecute. In spite of several high-profile convictions
for computer-related crimes, many types of cybercrime remain unpunished by legal consequences and laws and policies. Cybercrime, which is characterized by a very high number
of small perpetrators, poses particular legal difficulties because of this legal ambiguity. The
annual cost of cybercrime, difficult to measure because of its global and varied nature, is
likely underestimated and underreported[4].
Vigilance is essential in the battle against attackers, as they often target individual users
directly. Recent data from the Federal Trade Commission(FTC) reveals that in 2022, consumers reported fraud losses close to $8.8 billion, marking an increase of over 30 percent
from the prior year.[8]. Overall, in 2022, FTC collected over 5.1 million reports, encompassing not only the aforementioned fraud cases but also identity theft incidents and complaints about various consumer issues like credit bureau and bank or lender problems. Of
these, there were more than 1.1 million reports of identity theft. Attackers use different
social engineering techniques to flood the internet with deceptive websites daily, luring
victims into payments or disclosing sensitive info. Sometimes, attacks even come via traditional methods like phone calls or texts[106]. The variety of attack methods indicates
these are just initial signs of a larger problem, making it a challenging task to effectively
counter these threats.
In this digitally-driven era, the onus is on individuals, organizations, and governments
to actively engage in cybersecurity efforts, implementing effective defenses and staying
vigilant against the constant threat of cyber attackers. Continuous research efforts are cru2

cial to stay ahead of attackers and maintain a comprehensive understanding of cyber threats
over time.
1.1.1

Phishing

Phishing, a prevalent cybercrime method, deceives victims into exposing sensitive data
through misleading emails or fake websites, with attackers aiming for financial gain[41].
While it might seem simple and easy to mitigate, phishing attacks are persistent, evolving
in sophistication and volume.
Despite advances in anti-phishing measures integrated into browsers and implemented
through multi-layered security strategies[111, 72], these attacks remain a threat. The AntiPhishing Working Group (APWG)[6], established in 2003, continues to combat this issue,
with detected phishing incidents drastically rising over the years.
Phishing has been extensively studied and analyzed across the cybersecurity community. Numerous research papers, articles, and reports have delved into its various facets,
from attack methodologies to prevention and mitigation strategies[37, 38, 43, 47]. The
breadth of this research has led to a comprehensive understanding of how phishing attacks
are orchestrated, the psychological tactics employed by attackers, and the types of information they typically target.
Over the years, scholars and cybersecurity professionals have developed a robust body
of knowledge on phishing, categorizing different types of phishing attacks, such as spear
phishing[29], vishing[49], and smishing[117], and analyzing their unique characteristics.
This comprehensive collection of research underscores the cybersecurity community’s dedication to fully understanding and addressing phishing threats, demonstrating a profound
and well-rounded grasp of the subject. Such collective insights lay a solid groundwork
for ongoing and future initiatives to combat phishing attacks and safeguard users’ sensitive
data.

Fraudulent E-commerce Websites

Attackers also set their sights on everyday internet users, with the Federal Trade Commission in the United States receiving 2.8 million fraud reports in 2021, resulting in a
staggering $5.8 billion in financial losses. The top three reported fraud categories were impostor scams, online shopping scams, and reward/prize scams, accounting for $2.3 billion
in losses, nearly double that of 2020.
Although researchers have explored countermeasures to mitigate the impact of this type
of social engineering attack, most efforts have been directed at specific attack vectors, such
as fake online stores[25], pet scams[86], and charity scams[35].
These scams fall under a broader category we term fraudulent e-commerce websites
(FCWs). Unlike phishing attacks aiming to steal user information, FCWs trick users into
paying for nonexistent or misrepresented items or services. FCWs may not always impersonate well-known brands; instead, they often replicate the look and feel of legitimate
e-commerce sites to deceive users. Given the vast array of FCWs that users might encounter, there is a pressing need for broader, more effective defenses capable of detecting
generic FCWs.
A holistic approach to detecting FCWs at an ecosystem level is still lacking. The challenges impeding large-scale, ecosystem-level detection are manifold. Safeguarding users
from phishing attacks is challenging, with many issues still unresolved. While technical
countermeasures play a crucial role in providing automated, large-scale protection, they
are not a complete solution. Firstly, there is a noticeable absence of a current, publicly accessible dataset tailored for FCW detection, hindering the application of machine-learning
techniques. Past research often relied on spam datasets like spamscatter [17] or non-public
datasets [50, 58]. However, these sources are inadequate for FCW detection as less than
30% of URLs in spam emails correspond to FCWs [17]. Without proper filtering methods,

classifiers trained on this data are prone to inaccuracies due to noise. To accurately represent the current landscape, it is crucial to compile a contemporary and precise dataset of
Fraudulent e-Commerce Websites (FCWs). However, this is a daunting task as these websites often vanish quickly. Furthermore, FCWs do not rely on a single distribution method,
such as emails, making them harder to collect.
The content of FCWs is vital for detection so simple web crawling techniques might
not yield a suitable dataset for studying FCWs. Additionally, the data must exclusively
contain verified FCWs, and manually checking each one is a tedious and expert-intensive
task. FCWs also tend to change over time. In the past, fake online shops would lure
customers with extremely low prices. Nowadays, they are more likely to offer reasonable
prices, adapting to users who have grown skeptical of deals that seem too good to be true.
